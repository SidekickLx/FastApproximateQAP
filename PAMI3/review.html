Reviewer: 1

Recommendation: Author Should Prepare A Major Revision For A Second Review

Comments:
Strong points:
-- Good empirical evaluation

Weak points:
-- Not clear exactly what the novelty is compared to similar approaches
-- Overly informal writing style and presentation

Detailed comments:

I find the writing of the paper could be substantially improved. The style is way too conversational and familiar. e.g. "that FAQ was not always the best was annoying to us." Try to write the paper using standard academic voice. I'd also like to see more focus on the introduction and technical content, rather than focusing the paper so strongly on experimental results as you do now.

I'm not really sure why the authors chose to focus so much on brain graphs, even to the point of promoting it to the title. It seems like this is really a paper about proposing a novel approach to quadratic assignment algorithms, and brain graphs is simply one possible domain. Therefore I'd change the title be more general.

I'd also change the abstract: it tells me you have good results, but does little to say why your method is novel or important. The fact that you have good results should be mentioned, but should hardly be the main focus of your abstract in my opinion.

Section 5.1 doesn't add much. The complexity of Linear Assignment has been studied elsewhere, and Erdos Renyi graphs may not have representative performance of the models typical complexity.

In short, while the results of this paper seem to be good, I don't know that this algorithm warrants full publication in a journal. I'd think it'd be better suited to a shorter format -- unless more technical details, or a better explanation of its novelty are given.

Reviewer: 2

Recommendation: Revise and resubmit as “new”

Comments:
The key insight behind the approach is to allow the assignment matrix
to be continuous during optimization. During the optimization, the
objective is linearized (akin to convex concave procedure) and then
solved by the Hungarian method.

I am surprised that this technique is new given that the Lagrangian
relaxation technique has been used quite extensively for integer
programs. A google search leads me to the following link:

http://www.opt.math.tu-graz.ac.at/~cela/papers/qap_bericht.pdf

And there are a few linearization techniques described there. It'd be
great for the readers to know how is this technique compared to those
techniques.

The paper claims that the method is a "fast approximation" but the
results seem to be quite speculative. In particular, the speed
comparison in Figure 3 does not look impressive at all. Results in 5.8
need a baseline. Without any improvement in speed nor theoretical
guarantees, it's quite hard to acknowledge the contributions of this
work.

Reviewer: 3

Recommendation: Revise and resubmit as “new”

Comments:
Graph matching is a famously computational difficult problem. It’s clearly meaningful to design a fast algorithm for it. The manuscript proposed a fast approximate quadratic assignment problem algorithm (FAQ) for graph matching problem. The method showed some interesting results. The manuscript is well written and easy to follow. However, I have some major concerns.

Major concerns

1. No theoretical foundation for the algorithm is given in the manuscript. It seems that the manuscript only propose a heuristic algorithm, and does not present why the algorithm can solve the graph matching problem. Can the algorithm guarantee convergence? If the algorithm converges, is it convergent to the solution of the original problem? What are the error bounds of FAQ?
2. In section 5.1, the manuscript states that the FAQ algorithm is in cubic time based on experiments. A theoretical explanation should be presented.
3. Some synthetic data can be generated to show the performance of the algorithm, since the 137 benchmarks have limitations in the number of vertices. It might be interesting to see how the methods perform for graphs with specific properties (e.g. scale-free).
4. From Figure 4, although the linear fits showed FAQ is better than PATH, The time spent by FAQ has much larger variance than PATH. This figure shows running time by FAQ is rather unstable and unpredictable. From the figure, it is also unconvincing to say FAQ might be better when the number of vertices increase. The readers might want to see the experimental results which can prove this point even based on synthetic data.
5. In table 1, why not show the results by methods U and QCV?
6. In the left panel of figure 5, the algorithm U performs strangely bad (almost 100% error). Some explanation should be provided.


Minors:
1. ‘Table ??’ in the caption in Table 2.
2. P7,line8, something wrong with this sentence ‘If FAQ ….than FAQ’.
3. Page 11, line 17, ‘error is the fraction of vertices correctly matched’?
